The Rise of The Court of Public Opinion






Emergence of Cancel Culture


From the very beginning, communication and socialization over the Internet has been notoriously different in comparison to interacting with other people in real life. Whether you're emailing a work colleague some important information in a formal matter or you are posting some controversial opinions on Twitter through an anonymous account, we tend to behave differently on the Internet, even if only slightly. However, the more popularized perspective of Internet communications is the idea that annonymity and inconsequentialism go hand in hand in affecting how we use the Internet differently, and not always for the better. Since the beginning, there have always been people who used the Internet to troll, cyberbully, and harass others, all under the guise of anonymity.

Cancel culture is a recently-dubbed means by which the masses, or majority of Internet users on a platform, will hold individuals accountable through means such as excessive emphasis of the individual's behavior, seeking out personal information on the individual, and attempting to affect the real life of that individual behind the screen. Cancel culture is a controversial topic, as many believe it is the best way to hodl individuals accountable and regulate Internet behavior, while others see it as excessive, overexaggerated, and too harmful for the individuals involved. The biggest flaw to cancel culture is how mob mentality can devolve into being the loudest voice, despite conflicting factual information or lies made up about an individual. Typically when a person is "cancelled," they may lose sponsorships, careers, future job opportunities, and even close relationships, which can end up permanently affecting somebody's life, which can result from a large enough group of people declaring that the actions somebody has taken require that person to be cancelled. While cancel culture is one method to holding Internet users accountable, I believe that it can be misused so easily that it can cause permanent damage to somebody's life that I don't think that it should become the norm for managing Internet behavior.
